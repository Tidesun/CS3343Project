BELT & ROAD SERIES
Malaysia: Where People Speak Multiple Languages

Text and photos: Yen Leung,
Law

Hello! 你好！汝好無？(Lı́ hó bô?) Apa khabar? Kopivosian!

This is how we Malaysians greet each other in different languages. Yes, we are the ones who speak multiple languages, thanks to the multi-ethnic and multi-racial environment we live in. As a Malaysian Chinese, I frequently encountered questions like: why are you not black like others? How you know so many languages? How are you, Malays (馬來人)?

Want to know the answers? You’re at the right place. Here’s a brief ‘lecture’ about culture and races in Malaysia.

Who exactly make up Malaysia?

Malaysia is made up of two parts, namely the East (formerly known as North Borneo) and the West (also known as Peninsula). During prehistoric time, the West region was inhabited by aboriginal people, but this was not long before the Portuguese (1511) and the Dutch (1641) invaded Melaka. In less than a decade, however, the British colonized most territories in the West and East region.


The Buda Castle (Source: Malaysia facts.co)

To fulfill the colonial economy, southern Indian came to Peninsula work on plantations, especially rubber. Meanwhile, the British labour policy and Opium Wars (鴉片戰爭) had resulted in massive influx of southern China into the Peninsula to work as tin miners. This is why Malaysia today consists of three large racial groups (Malay, Chinese, Indian).

After the invasion by the Japanese during the WWII and resumption of power by the British, the Peninsula gained independence and formed the Federation of Malaya on 31st August 1957. Yeap, it was Malaya, not Malaysia. The current Federation of MALAYSIA was only formed by the Malaya, North Borneo and Singapore on 16 September 1963. Sadly, Singapore left the federation a year later.

As you can read from above, Chinese have moved to Malaysia in the early century. We were born and have been raised in Malaysia for a few generations. Chinese = Malaysians ≠ Malays. See the difference? This is why we have different skin colour from the Malays. So next time when you spot a Malaysian Chinese, stop asking him/her why he/she speaks Chinese.

Apart from the main racial groups, there are other ethnics including Kadazan, Bajau, Minangkabau, Bapa-Nyonya, Punjabis, Murut, Iban, Melanau and Ulu. Believing in ‘Satu Malaysia’ (One Malaysia), we love having each other. Although conflict does happens, deep inside we can’t deny what makes Malaysia special is the rich culture.


(Source: Fullnoor)

Malaysians = Singaporeans and Indonesians? NOPE!

Do note that although we have similar culture with Singapore and Indonesia, this does not make us the same people. We have different lifestyles and languages that people from other places might not notice.

Each country has its own uniqueness, same goes to Malaysia. While Malaysians and Singaporeans can speak similar languages, we have different accent. If you listen carefully enough, Malaysians speak Chinese with tone variations influenced by dialects and languages. Meanwhile, their counterparts in Singapore learn Chinese which is geared more towards the pinyin pronunciation. Although Malaysians and Indos speak Malay, we use mixed languages more often in one sentence, while Indos mostly speak Malay and English.

What do Malaysians like?

Bahasa Rojak

When it comes to Malaysians, the most symbolic thing you may notice is we use ‘Bahasa Rojak’ always, meaning we use multiple languages and dialects in one sentence.

Example 1: “bro, long time no see leh! 吃飯都不jio我” (hi bro, long time no see. You never invited me to have meal together)

Example 2: “sangat 好吃 eh this food, later tapau more!” (this food is very delicious. Let’s order more for take-away)

Such fascinating language hybrid is largely attributable to colonial powers and trade with Arabic. Perhaps such value of “more is better than one” is an intuitive idea to us so that we are able to communicate with all other ethnics group. Bahasa Rojak is a uniquely adaptable language that Malaysians own together.

Yam Cha（飲茶）

Malaysians love yam cha, but this is not the ordinary dim sum in Hong Kong. Yam cha in Malaysia means hanging out casually with friends. So, yam cha can be having fish noodle as breakfast, enjoying chicken wings and satay at a pasar for tea, drinking avacado juice or coconut drink by the beach during dawn, or even eating roti canai at Mamak in the midnight. With a pair of slippers, t-shirt and shorts, you are good to go.

Festival? It’s holiday time!

One thing I particularly like about Malaysia is the holidays. Public holidays across different states together make up a 50 days period of holiday! The main reason behind this is, again, the various ethnics group we have. We respect different ethnics and religions, so we celebrate together their special festivals, including the Chinese New Year, Hari Raya (Muslim New Year), Thaipusam (Indian), Harvest Festival (Kadazan), Christmas (Christians) and Wesak Day (Buddhist).

We have MORE than nasi lemak and curry

Thanks to the distinct ethnics, we do not only get holidays, we also get to eat myriad of cuisines. Apart from nasi lemak, curry and bak kut teh, there are various mouth-watering delicacies. Seafood, laksa, rendang, satay, bamboo rice, grilled cuttlefish, traditional cakes, roti canai...I can’t possibly list out all the amazing food here. So, pay a visit to Malaysia and fill your hungry stomach! Here are two of my all time favourites.

Indeed, take a break and relax at the Cameron Highlands with fresh strawberries and tea, or grab a coconut and sit by the beach waiting for sunset. No jokes, they are simply mesmerizing.

One-belt-one-road

Just in case you think this is no more than a buzzword, OBOR is a strategy to foster economic and diplomatic relationships across Asia, Africa and Europe. It was initiated by China in 2013 and has covered more than 60 countries in the plan.

Malaysia is strategically located along the OBOR route. In particular, West Malaysia possesses major ports that are accessible by important international maritime route. Large-scale infrastructures are the focus of Malaysia-OBOR, including the East Coast Rail Line project and port infrastructure construction. The OBOR project is expected to shower abundant trade and employment opportunities in sectors spanning trade, investment, tourism, education, financial and infrastructure.

Let’s learn some fun vocab to end your virtual tour of Malaysia

In Malaysia, we use one word to represent various things. The most common examples are ‘paper bag’ and ‘water’.

　　

There are also some slangs that you gotta know!

Walao = an exclamation of shock / surprise / amazement / angry / disbelief
Cincai = whatever
Abuden = a sarcastic remark used for people who say something very obvious
Boss = asides from the general meaning of ‘employer’, this is a word used to describe the workers or customers at food stalls
Gostan = reverse a vehicle

If you would like to know more about Malaysia, feel free to approach the CityU Malaysian Community. We are nice people, I guarantee.
