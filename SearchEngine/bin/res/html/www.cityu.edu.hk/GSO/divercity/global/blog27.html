13 April 2018

From Diver to Scientist: My Journey Exploring the Underwater World

Text and photos: Walter Dellisanti,
Biomedical Sciences

Design: Yumeng Zhu,
Creative Media

Before I came to Hong Kong, I grew up diving into the deep blue and discovering the depth of the ocean, swimming through rocky creeks and gliding onto the corrugated sandy bottom. Many times, I dove deep into the ocean in the hope that I could discover ancient wrecks lying on the bottom. The Mediterranean Sea is one of the world’s unique hotspots, a cradle of biodiversity where three continents meet and share the beauty of this place, where European, African, and Eastern cultures combine.

    
Two sights of the Mediterranean Sea: Miramare’s castle looks over the Marine Protected Area of Miramare in the Adriatic Sea, Italy (left); Ports des Canonge, Mallorca Island, Spain (right).

My love for the ocean brought me to pass through the Strait of Gibraltar into the Atlantic Ocean some years ago. Angel sharks (Squatina squatina) flew over the waves competing with other inhabitants of the sea; curious trumpet-fish (Aulostomus strigosus) saw human divers as strange animals that made bubbles underwater; and many different colorful parrot-fish (Sparisoma euscarus cretense) looked for something to crunch.

Over the course of my career in marine biology research, I have sailed through stormy seas, made toasts with sailors, and enjoyed warm sunsets down to the horizon. From time to time, these experiences remind me of the place where I took my first underwater breath many years ago: a coral reef in the Red Sea. The Red Sea was considered to be one of the best diving spots for European divers in early 2000s, when diving tourism blossomed in many Egyptian coastal areas. My life changed at the moment I discovered this new blue world teeming with strange life: fish, sea urchins, sea stars, sharks … I will never forget that feeling of excitement as I discovered this beautiful underwater world.

Over the years, I have been lucky enough to have a number of opportunities to dive in several locations around the globe, including the largest center of biodiversity in the world: the Coral Triangle. I flew to Indonesia for the Reef Check Italia EcoDiver course. At Bangka Island, North Sulawesi, I saw a different sea and a coral reef that was arduously fighting a battle against climate change and maintaining its ecological strength (see photos).

The Indonesian dives were a turning point in my life. Because of that experience, I made up my mind to become a reef eco-diver, applying the techniques to save coral reefs.

    
Reef life in Bangka Island, North Sulawesi, Indonesia.

Since I first landed in Hong Kong, I have heard various opinions (and no doubt more are still to come) about this international hub of Asia. The first: this is such an incredible city! Everything seems to be perfect, and the city gives you a feeling that each of your dreams is achievable. The second: there are at least five diving shops (based on what I could see), and there should be great underwater interest to satisfy such an economy! However, with so many people living here, where does all the trash go? What is the underwater environment like here? As a diver and a researcher, I want to go deeper, to go diving and see what the bottom of the ocean is like here.

As a result, I successfully applied for the PhD program in Biomedical Sciences at CityU. This opportunity gives me access to the State Key Laboratory in Marine Pollution, which was recently established at CityU. As a real underwater scientist, I can apply my knowledge working with the laboratory and in saving Hong Kong’s corals. Would you like to experience the thrill of breathing underwater and working with me to save our beautiful ocean? We have so many things to explore!

The Hong Kong coral communities consist of more than one hundred species, with an undefined number of fish and invertebrates. My commitment is to understand why these tiny animals, a combination of many invisible organisms (living in so-called symbiosis), are living in such a marginal environment. Hong Kong is so warm and so cold, so saline and so sweet! It does not have the tropical weather that boosts coral life and growth, but somehow they survive! With particles such as sand constantly floating in the sea and the not-so-breathable air, this marine ecosystem could be like hell for coral life. So how do these organisms survive? Answering this question is the aim of the research that I am carrying out with my team.

If you are not familiar with diving, but this article has you excited about exploring the underwater world, here is what you can do. First, if you are not a diver yet, you will need diving training, after which you will be certified as an open water diver. You will then be able to dive with a buddy all around the world.

Fascinating, isn’t it?

So, put a tank on your back, take a giant stride, and… let’s go diving together!
