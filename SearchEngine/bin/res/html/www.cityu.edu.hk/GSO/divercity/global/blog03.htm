4 February 2014

International Debating Competition in India — A Country of Diversity

Text and photos: Bryce Hartley,
Linguistics and Language Teaching

Part of what makes Hong Kong and CityU such wonderful places is the diversity. While most people know Hong Kong is where East meets West, many people do not know that Hong Kong has also been home to many Indians for more than a century. Also, now at CityU one of the biggest groups of international students is from India. The presence of Indians in Hong Kong is not surprising given the long historical ties between India and China. Furthermore, the two countries hold the place for the top two fastest growing economies in the world.

This year the 34th World Universities Debating Championship (WUDC) was hosted by Rajalakshmi Institutions in Chennai India. WUDC includes more than 400 teams from 250 universities representing over 70 different nations, making it the biggest international debating competition and one of the biggest and most diverse student organized activities in the world. This year CityU was lucky enough to take part in this tournament, sending myself and other teammates to compete on behalf of CityU.


WUDC provided ample opportunity to not only debate, but also learn from experienced debaters, make friends from around the world and explore the great nation of India. With more than a thousand participants in total it took the efforts of so many different people to make the tournament happen and is testament to how much we can accomplish working together. For the main round, there were eight debates and each team is randomly assigned a role they have to represent (for or against the motion) and the motion to be debated is announced only 15 minutes before the debate begins. What this does is force people to think about issues they might have never thought of and from perspectives they might disagree with. In an often polarized world with dangerous misconceptions, debates like this give people the chance to see how one issue can be viewed and interpreted in so many different ways. Such a skill set is valuable in any university and especially in an international institution like CityU because it is imperative we can understand the different opinions and ideas coming from our diverse student body and professors.


During my short stay in India I had the chance to get a glimpse into the country’s diversity and hospitality. Everybody I met from fellow debaters to rickshaw drivers were truly welcoming and helpful. Their hospitable ways is part of what allowed me to learn about how diverse their home is. Linguistically India has more than 24 languages and English is one of the official government languages. As a result talking to most people was not difficult, allowing for deeper discussion. The religious landscape of India is also very complex with Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism originating in India and now today also includes countless others. One of the great byproducts of so many different cultures is the rich array of cuisines India offers. Contrary to popular belief, not all Indian food is spicy and in fact many Indians do not like spicy food. Another interesting aspect is while the West prefers fork and knife and East Asia prefers chopsticks, Indians traditionally use their hands to eat. While seemingly easy, eating certain foods with your hands, like rice, takes skill and practice.


Best of all if you want to get a taste of India for yourself inexpensive flights can be found with low cost carriers such as AirAsia and SpiceJet. Hong Kong’s extensive transportation network on land, air and sea makes visiting other parts of Asia both convenient and not too costly. Apart from travelling, you may also consider the student exchange programme offered by CityU’s partner institution, India Institute of Technology in Delhi to have a more in-depth understanding of the rich cultural heritage of India.

Also, while still in Hong Kong it is easy to learn about other cultures, like India, by joining clubs and societies on and off campus, which give students opportunities to interact with our diverse student body and the community in Hong Kong. Additionally, many clubs and societies (like WUDC) organize events to go outside Hong Kong that provides more chances to see what the world holds. So be involved and take advantage of all the opportunities CityU has to offer.
