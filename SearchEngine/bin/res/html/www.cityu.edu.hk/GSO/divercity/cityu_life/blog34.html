From One Student to Another: Sharing Culture through Language

Text: Natasya Viona Chandra,
Quantitative Finance & Risk Management

Photos: Cocoon Chengyi Cao,
Marketing

Interviewees:

Abigail Lee,
College of Science & Engineering

Candy Ching-i Fu,
College of Science & Engineering

Nilar Myat Aung,
College of Veterinary Medicine and Life Sciences

Yvette Shuk-wai Yung,
Media and Communication

As an international student living in Hong Kong, one of the toughest challenges to tackle is the language barrier. For us whose mother tongue does not include tones in the language, learning the 4 tones for Mandarin is hard enough, let alone the 9 tones incorporated in Cantonese. During freshmen year, I was filled with anxiety every time I had to order at the canteen because sometimes the chef asked for my order in Cantonese. But lo and behold, more initiatives have been brought up by the university to help non-local students like me!

One of these initiatives is the Global Mixer (G-Mixer) programme led by the GSO. Over the years, it has become a regular programme that is offered every year. In this programme, a team of local students join as G-mixers dedicated to promoting campus diversity and integration by organising activities for non-local students, such as Survival Cantonese Class and a series of cool and fun activities to enrich students’ cultural exposure.


The Graduating Class of Survival Cantonese 2017

Survival Cantonese Class is a series of classes held every week by G-mixers with the aim to teach non-local students basic phrases in Cantonese in order to help them ‘survive’ in Hong Kong, particularly in handling conversations with non-English speakers in daily life. This semester’s Survival Cantonese classes have ended, and the G-mixers held a graduation party with programme participants. It’s the perfect platform for a reunion between the local and non-local who were involved in the programme!

I had the opportunity to ask the organisers and participants regarding how the G-mixer programme have impacted them and their university life. Here’s what they have to say:

This year, the G-Mixer programme recruits a lot of freshmen for the job. What intrigued you to join the programme?

I think they promote this programme really well and that was what caught my eye in the first place. As this is my first year in university, I wanted to make a lot of friends, especially with non-local students! I grew up with in a local Hong Kong community, so I wanted to get to know different cultures by making friends with international students.

What’s your personal goal in joining this programme?

I wanted to take up opportunities and learn things outside of my comfort zone. I see senior G-mixers being able to adapt to emergency situations and be comfortable to be assigned to positions handling a variety of tasks, so I believe I’ll be able to achieve a lot through such experience.


From being teachers to being MCs, G-mixers are challenged to try out new things and develop new skills!

What was the challenge you encountered when organising the Survival Cantonese classes?

Talking in English! We carried the burden of accurately translating from Cantonese to English, and sometimes we were scared that our participants would not be able to understand us well if we did not articulate ourselves precisely in English. There are also words that we use in a particular context and sometimes our participants would ask questions like “Why do you use this word instead of this”. We couldn’t really answer these questions because that’s just how Cantonese is and some phrases are lost in translation!

What was the most interesting moment you have experienced in this programme?

We had a game in which participants had to use phrases in Cantonese, but they sounded like singing rather than speaking! As native speakers, we never would have expected our language to sound like a song because we have been familiar with all the tones.


“Learning a new language is tough, but we try to make it as enjoyable as possible!”

What was your takeaway from this experience?

Apart from having to be flexible in organising events, I also learnt to be cautious of what we say to international students. They have different cultural backgrounds and different points of view towards stuff, like politics, for example. I was brought to be aware that I might have been too comfortable in saying certain things out of my mind in the past, and that when facing participants with different backgrounds, I had to learn to be cautious that some personal view points could be inappropriate if said to others.

Did you enjoy your time being a G-mixer? What is the advice you have to people that might be interested in being a G-mixer in the future?

Yes! I’ve been a G-mixer for only a semester so far, but I gained a lot of experience and friends during my time here, and I will continue to be a G-mixer until I graduate! For new G-mixers, my advice is “don’t be shy”! You need to be energetic, active and confident. Being proactive is the key! Accept more opportunities and as cliché, as it sounds, just step out of your comfort zone and don’t be afraid to speak to new people. Only then you will experience something new!


Non-local students not only learn a new language, but they also make tons of new friends!

Apart from G-mixers, I also got the chance to sit with the participants of the Survival Cantonese class to see how they have benefitted from the lessons.

How was Survival Cantonese class?

It was fun! It’s like a nice social event where we can meet new people and learn Cantonese at the same time! I made friends with the G-mixer who led our group, and the classes were beneficial and practical for daily life as well, especially in the canteen!


“Can I get a bowl of fish balls, please?”

What did you learn about the local community?

Hong Kong people have different tones that they make to express their feelings. It’s interesting to see how they speak to each other. We’re taking baby steps to learn Cantonese, but we’re sure we’ll get there someday!
