2 March 2018

My Cantonese Teacher Yin Jie

Text: Asad Raza,
Mechanical and Biomedical Engineering

Photos: Andy Yang,
Business Economics

Design: Yumeng Zhu,
Creative Media

The glass doors flung open, and I entered the hall lobby waiting for the lift while sifting through my LinkedIn feed, a soft voice echoes in my ears from the right: “Jo Sun”. I turned my head 90 degrees to the right to see if anyone was talking to me. “Good Morning!” A lady with a luminous grin on her face greeted me.

Eliciting a friendly smile, I greeted her back: “Hello, Good Morning!” Whereupon she immediately retorted with the following improvisation: “Welcome to Hall 5! I am ‘Yin Jie’. “What??”, I asked her name without paying any attention to the “Welcome to Hall 5” part.

Since it had hardly been a week in Hong Kong, I was not acquainted (not even barely) with the culture, let alone the names. “YY-ii-nn JJ-aay”, she reiterated. I still couldn’t get it. Eyeballs turned, and eye-brows raised, I moron-ishly asked:” I am sorry, again?”. “Yin Jie, Yin Jie… YY-ii-nn JJ-aay”, she reiterated for requisite number of times and slowed down to help me get the pronunciation right. “Ohh! Yin Jie?”, I asked timidly this time because I couldn’t afford to make any more mistakes after Yin Jie tried so hard to help me. I got it right this time. “Yin Jie!” it was. It is actually a nickname, meaning “Sister Yin” in Cantonese.

Name resolved. Before going to the “Welcome to Hall 5” part, let’s decrypt the “Jo Sun” code first. “So, you just said ‘Jo Sun’ just now?”

I solemnly pledge that I got this one just as right in the first attempt. This, I reckon, might contribute to dilute the magnitude of my incognizance with the language or to pick up a new language fast. “Chung Ming lah! Yes! I said ‘Joo-saan’, and it means ‘Good Morning’” said Yin Jie. “Wait! But would you be good enough one more time to tell me what is ‘Chung… Ming’?”, I wondered. To my fortune, she continued: “’Chung Ming’ means clever lah. Youuu learn language very quickly”.

    
I feel lucky to have met a nice person like Yin Jie who shared kindness, warmth and her local language knowledge to help a student like myself adapt to a new language environment.

Did you notice that pesky little ‘lah’ that was used at the end of many sentences (at least up till this point in time)? Well! If you didn’t, you should have been attentive enough. As I talked to more people in Hong Kong, I realized I need to make peace with this ubiquitous “lah” and master the use of it because this magical word appears to be used in many different ways.

“What’s this ‘lah’ by the way?” I asked. Yin Jie smiled and just like thinking about how to explain the essence of her mother tongue, she made a few gestures randomly with her hands while thinking of a way to make it plain for me. “’Laaah…’, ‘Lah’ means nothing—it is an exclamation mark”. That wasn’t a very convincing explanation to me in the first place, but later a local friend of mine made it analogous with ‘bro’ or ‘Dude’ in English, and I felt like I have digested that somehow.

Since that day until now, every time I walk in or out of the hall, Yin Jie would say something in Cantonese and tell me the meaning of different words. Later, she would very cleverly test how well I learnt by building upon a new sentence with the words she taught me earlier. Because of the efforts Yin Jie made and her kindness, out of moral responsibility, I tried very hard to memorize these words to pass my very own “surprise tests”, so as to keep my dignity, as an amateur Chinese speaker and Yin Jie’s student, intact. However, there’s a huge conundrum: whatever Chinese I listen from one ear, goes out of the other.

Dude! Chinese is difficult. Or, my memory doesn’t live up to my expectations. Whichever the case may be, a simple solution is to write the words down. So, here’s a sneak-peek into my cheat sheet:

Now, have you noticed that wisdom behind the list that helps me memorize the pronunciation of the terms? It’s all from Yin Jie. Not just that, she speaks English (without shying away) better than many security guards I’ve come across so far in Hong Kong. This lady is always so contented with life and blushing with glamor that my (occasional) depression of staying behind on assignments sort of goes away when I talk to her. Whenever I am in the lobby, I have hardly seen anyone who can walk out of the hall, escaping her “Jo San”, blended with a cheerful smile. It seems to me that this lady doesn’t push herself to pursue happiness, instead, she ensues it from making others happy.

“I have worked for Hall 5 for fifteen years, and I love my job”, she exclaims with pride and contentment.

One can learn, from this lady, the much needed ‘principles of contentment’.


With Yin Jie’s presence, Hall 5 feels more like home.
