22 June 2018

Hong Kong Diaries: The Tales of an Exchange Student Told through Art

Text:
Sadhika Nanda,
Asian and International Studies

Akos Schneider,
visiting research student from Moholy-Nagy University of Art and Design Budapest

Illustration: Bianka Parázs,
exchange student from Moholy-Nagy University of Art and Design Budapest

Design: Ching-man Leung,
Creative Media

Those of us who have had the pleasure of going on exchange know that it is one of the nicest experiences college life has to offer. We are guilty of having spoken about our exchange semester non-stop, posting photograph after photograph on Instagram and dedicating long essays to go with our new Facebook profile pictures from different parts of the world.

As a Creative Media major, Bianka Parázs, one of our very own exchange students at CityU from Hungary, decided to showcase her exchange experience in a truly unique fashion at the end of her semester-long journey here in Hong Kong.

In collaboration with a local cafe in Prince Edward, Bianka curated a week-long exhibition of her artwork inspired by her study abroad experience. Opening on May 14th, 2018 at BOUND by Hillywood, the exhibition was aptly titled “Hong Kong Diaries” and showcased a variety of ink-based illustrations — snippets of Bianka’s life as an exchange student in Hong Kong.

When asked what catalysed this effort, she says, “When you travel to a new place, (usually) you experience some kind of culture shock but in Hong Kong, you experience a colour shock followed by a gastronomical shock”. The solo artist says she only ever made black and white sketches before she came to Hong Kong and the sights and sounds of this city filled colours into her work.

In Bianka’s Hong Kong Diaries, one sees images that resonate: the chaos of Sham Shui Po, the calm of Stanley’s beaches and the dichotomy of chaos and calm in Tai O. Interestingly, the artist’s first ever sketch was of a place that truly hits home for all of us — CityU’s Chinese Garden - a traditional, quiet oasis amidst CityU’s buildings.


“Stanley Beach” by Bianka Parázs

Bianka says she found our gardens inspirational in style and very memorable, “I refer to the Chinese Gardens as the Froggy Garden because you can always hear a croak or two, no matter how silent it is!”, she says jokingly. For Bianca, CityU inspired her in many different ways: from providing the right infrastructure to create, to being open-minded and conducive to creativity. She narrates, “The CMC [Run Run Shaw Creative Media Centre] has everything you would ever need — of which, the most important part is the teachers. They encourage you and they help you open your mind — and not just in class. For instance, I could never imagine that I would work out with one of my teachers, but I did! It is these experiences that mark my time in Hong Kong”. Bianka also notes that she found her fellow classmates to be particularly open-minded, as so many of them have been on exchange themselves, which helps students expand their world vision, just as it did hers.

Bianka’s unique works of art epitomise the spirit of a traveller in Hong Kong. They encapsulate the emotion this city evokes, while capturing the charm of being an exchange student. It is safe to say that Hong Kong has made a mark on Bianka and that Bianka, through her art, has made her mark on Hong Kong.


“Fa Hui Park” by Bianka Parázs

Apart from exploring the uniqueness of Hong Kong, Bianka also made an effort to see as much of Asia as she could in one semester and to document her feelings and her way of seeing the different places through her drawings and illustrations.


“Seoul” by Bianka Parázs

Akos Schneider, a visiting research student at CityU from Hungary, delivered the opening speech of Bianca’s exhibition, “Beyond Culture Shock – The Hong Kong Diaries of Bianka Parázs”:

“When you move to a new city – especially when you move to a new continent – you tend to over-operate. The operational mode of life kicks in: with bank accounts, application forms, rental contracts, expat forums, MRT maps, schedules, and appointments. This transitional period after the so-called “arrival” can take longer than you think – to settle down at a new place takes much more than just to step out of an airplane.

And there is also this other thing… I never really understood what culture shock means, but I certainly understand the excitement and the tornado-like perceptional state that gets you, when you radically move away from your center and get exposed to something new yet unknown. Anthropologists call this state ‘liminality’: a threshold between the old and the new, where things you are familiar with don’t support you anymore, but new structures and frameworks are yet to emerge. You are in-between.

Bianka doesn’t seem to be shocked, nor is she over-operating. Her drawings dwell just fine in liminality. Irregular – sometimes even surreal – spaces, and the charm of unfinished details characterize her visual language. The vivid colors she used bring movement to the static drawings.

The “Hong Kong Diaries” is a dreamlike rewiring of the city and its surroundings. Metaphysical realism, one could say, but influenced by Japanese anime, mixed with ethno-Caribbean-afro-goddess vibes. The overused notion of urban jungle seems to find a fresh meaning in these drawings.

Bianka’s visual diary is influenced first and foremost by Hong Kong itself. I have never been to a more psychedelic place than this before. It is amazing how many layers are overlapping with each other. From the first day onwards, Bianka tried to immerse herself into this organic superstructure.

From the fishermen of Tai O, through the neon signs in Mong Kok and Jordan, to the hidden alleys of Sham Shui Po, the ‘diaries’ are not just a personal collection of memories, but also the representation of a personal epistemology.

To share them with the public – I think – is an honest way to say thank you to this amazing city and to the people who live here.”
