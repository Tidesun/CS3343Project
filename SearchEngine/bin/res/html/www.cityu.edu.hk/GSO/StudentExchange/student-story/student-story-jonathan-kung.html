    Kung Cheuk Hin, Jonathan Catholic University of Portugal, Portugal
    Feb 15
    On my first day in Lisbon, I travelled my school to view the city's highest point - St Jorge Castle. Apart from being known as the city with over 250 days of sunshine, Lisbon is also famed for its hilly landscape and colourful 19th century buildings. Everything is extremely vivid and lively in this sunshine! I highly suggest visiting with a camera during sunset. Better still, set your camera to time lapse to capture the beauty of this breathtaking city.
    I was glad to meet some exchange students during the student orientation. Jonathan and Felix are from Singapore and Germany, and were the first friends I made during orientation. To better understand our host city, we signed up for a bus tour to Belem to experience the traditional Lisbon. Lisbon has amazing historic sites, but Felix mentioned that St. Gallen, Switzerland - where he studies - is even better if you love nature. Felix’s recommendation will likely bring me to visit St. Gallen.
    Searching for directions in Lisbon is like being a detective – it can take hours to find the right address. Most buildings in Lisbon do not have an address plaque. Instead, the numbers can be all around you, including the floor. It is quite a fresh experience. When exploring Lisbon, you definitely have to keep an eye out for addresses, and in some cases, pickpockets.
    Mar 1
    My first ever trip to Portugal was travelling with my schoolmates to Porto, where Portugal’s best wine is manufactured. The tour brought Ross (my Scottish exchange mate) and myself to the Wine Museum. Here, we learnt about wine history and tasted the best wine of our visit. Say cheers, everyone!
    If you ever spend your semester in Lisbon (or other European cities), it is worth enrolling in the ERASMUS programme. This programme offers free tours and parties during your exchange months. The photo above features one of my favourite tours to Alfameda, and a complementary visit to St. Jorge Castle, which is the highest point of Lisbon. The tour guide offered some great recommendations on things to do here. Make sure you get his contact information if you choose to visit!
    Like most European cities, flea markets can be spotted everywhere. In some Lisbon metro stations, you might come across stalls selling traditional cork accessories and bags, which is the most common material used in Portugal. You can usually get great bargains for souvenirs in these markets – much cheaper than souvenir shops. These are definitely the best gifts for your friends and family!
    Mar 15
    Meals for three can be expensive at any restaurant in Lisbon. Therefore, my Korean friend, Myungi, suggested making a meal together at his home. Myungi made a simple-but-delicious Korean dish, which is similar to a Thai wrap, but with Korean hot sauce. It’s a great recipe if you don’t want to spend too much time cooking.
    Sunny weekends are the best time to explore. Today’s destination was Cabo da Roca, which is the most western coast of Portugal and the European continent. This was a great place to see the sunset and waves crashing against gigantic cliffs. I really enjoyed the fresh breeze and sunshine throughout the day.
    If you ever come to Portugal in February, you definitely should not miss the Samba parade on Carnival/Shrove Tuesday, which is also a holiday for Portugal and Brazil.
    The parade showcases the passion Brazilians have for dancing in the sunshine (and the cold!) You will see many different costumes worn by dancers, and just like the Cheung Chau Da Jiu Festival, children can also take part in the parade!
    Apr 1
    Pena Palace, listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is our destination for the week. Bright and sharp coloured walls are symbolic of this Romantic era building, which used to be a chapel for royals and officials. The layout and display of utensils inside the museum offers a throwback to the luxurious royal lifestyle. Interestingly, the traditional Portuguese white tiles do not clutter the main character of the building. As such, it offers a fresh experience if you’re bored of seeing tiles!

    Feira da Ladra is one of the best places to visit, as you can find great deals on antiques and souvenirs. There are numerous ‘stalls’ selling everything you can think of. However, this is also known as a place where thieves sell their stolen goods for cash. Yet, it is still a great place for tourists to visit and look for hidden ‘treasures’. Funnily enough, I came across some stalls that were selling HK$5 coins for 1 euro – interesting!
    Apr 15
    Seville is one of the most amazing places I have ever been so far! Small canals, with traditional Spanish architectural design, helps the Placa de España stand out among the other landmarks in the city. Seeing the fountains, arch bridges, and taking a boat ride is the best way to enjoy the sunset! It is definitely a fantastic way to view the tiles of each of the provinces in this plaza.
    Morocco is a definitely for you if you’re fed up with going to cosmopolitan cities. Taking advantage of the low fare and the Easter break, my mates and I set off on our journey from Fez, where the traditional color-dying products are made. We were invited to walk through the state-owned dying factory and witness the mixing of colours and products. The factory owners explained how each of the colours was extracted from various natural materials – so no artificial colours here! Because the dying colours are irritating to the nose, the guide gave us mint leaves to ease the smell.
    Most of the hostels in Morocco offer a complimentary breakfast to their guests. Here's a typical breakfast layout, with mainly bread along with different seasonings. Always look out for the most famous drink, mint tea, which refreshes your mind every morning. It is interesting to know that Moroccan people have bread at every meal. From breakfast to lunch, lunch to tea, and dinner as well. Amazingly, breads are so cheap here it’s nearly free here!
